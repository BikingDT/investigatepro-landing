Root cause analysis is only as good as the structure you bring to it. Without a framework, investigators often jump to conclusions, miss contributing factors, or identify symptoms instead of causes.

These templates provide that structure. Each one is designed for different scenarios and complexity levels. Use them as starting points and customize for your organization.

1. 5 Whys Analysis Template

Best for: Simple problems with a single cause chain. Quick investigations. Training new investigators.

The 5 Whys technique is deceptively simple: keep asking "why" until you reach the root cause. In practice, most problems need 3-7 iterations. Our template includes space for multiple branches when you hit forks in the causal chain.

Example 5 Whys Analysis

Problem: Worker received hand injury

Why 1: Hand contacted rotating equipment

Why 2: Guard was removed

Why 3: Guard interfered with cleaning task

Why 4: Cleaning procedure didn't account for guard

Why 5: Procedure not updated when equipment changed

Root Cause: No management of change process for equipment modifications

Template Features:

  • Linear and branching formats
  • Evidence documentation for each "why"
  • Root cause classification field
  • Corrective action recommendations
  • Available in Word, Excel, and PDF

2. Fishbone (Ishikawa) Diagram

Best for: Complex problems with multiple contributing factors. Team brainstorming sessions. Manufacturing and process issues.

The fishbone diagram organizes potential causes into categories. The classic 6M categories are: Man (People), Machine (Equipment), Method (Process), Material, Measurement, and Mother Nature (Environment). Our template lets you customize categories for your industry.

Template Features:

  • Pre-built 6M structure
  • Customizable category labels
  • Sub-cause branches
  • PowerPoint version for presentations
  • Fillable PDF for quick use

3. ICAM Investigation Template

Best for: Serious incidents. Regulatory requirements. Organizations committed to systemic improvement.

ICAM (Incident Cause Analysis Method) is the gold standard for high-risk industries. It explicitly focuses on organizational factors, not individual blame. This comprehensive template guides you through all ICAM elements including the PEEPO framework.

ICAM Template Sections:

  1. Incident Summary: What happened, when, where, who was involved
  2. Timeline: Sequence of events leading to the incident
  3. Absent/Failed Defences: Barriers that should have prevented it
  4. Individual/Team Actions: Immediate actions (not blame)
  5. Task/Environmental Conditions: Workplace factors
  6. Organizational Factors (PEEPO): Systemic issues
  7. Recommendations: Corrective and preventive actions

4. Fault Tree Analysis Template

Best for: System failures. Safety-critical industries. Quantitative risk analysis.

Fault Tree Analysis (FTA) works backwards from the undesired event (the "top event") to identify all possible causes using Boolean logic (AND/OR gates). It's particularly useful for understanding how multiple failures must combine to cause an incident.

Template Features:

  • AND/OR gate symbols
  • Basic event shapes
  • Probability calculation fields
  • Visio and PowerPoint versions
  • Minimal cut set identification

5. 8D Problem Solving Template

Best for: Quality issues. Customer complaints. Team-based problem solving. Manufacturing defects.

The 8D (Eight Disciplines) methodology was developed by Ford Motor Company. It's a structured approach that emphasizes team problem-solving and preventing recurrence. Our template walks you through all 8 disciplines with prompts and examples.

The 8 Disciplines:

  1. D1: Establish the Team
  2. D2: Describe the Problem
  3. D3: Interim Containment Actions
  4. D4: Root Cause Analysis
  5. D5: Permanent Corrective Actions
  6. D6: Implement & Validate
  7. D7: Prevent Recurrence
  8. D8: Recognize the Team

6. Pareto Analysis Chart

Best for: Prioritizing causes. Identifying the "vital few" factors. Data-driven investigations.

The Pareto principle (80/20 rule) states that roughly 80% of effects come from 20% of causes. This Excel template automatically generates Pareto charts from your data, helping you focus on the factors that matter most.

7. FMEA Template

Best for: Proactive risk assessment. Design reviews. Process improvements.

Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA) is primarily a proactive tool, but it's invaluable after an incident to systematically identify other potential failure modes. Our template calculates Risk Priority Numbers (RPN) automatically.

FMEA Columns:

  • Process step / Component
  • Potential failure mode
  • Potential effects
  • Severity (1-10)
  • Potential causes
  • Occurrence (1-10)
  • Current controls
  • Detection (1-10)
  • RPN (auto-calculated)
  • Recommended actions

8. Incident Report Form

Best for: Initial incident documentation. Regulatory reporting. Consistent data collection.

Before you can analyze root causes, you need accurate incident data. This incident report template captures all essential information: who, what, when, where, witnesses, immediate actions, and preliminary observations.

9. Corrective Action Tracker

Best for: Action follow-up. Accountability. Proving effectiveness of improvements.

Root cause analysis means nothing if corrective actions aren't implemented. This Excel tracker monitors action status, due dates, responsible parties, and verification of effectiveness.

10. Investigation Checklist

Best for: Ensuring completeness. Training new investigators. Standardizing your process.

Don't miss critical steps. This checklist covers the entire investigation lifecycle: scene preservation, evidence collection, interviews, analysis, documentation, and follow-up.

How to Choose the Right RCA Template

The most effective template is the one matched to your incident's complexity, your team's experience, and your regulatory requirements. Here's a practical decision guide:

Start with Severity

For minor incidents and near-misses, the 5 Whys template is usually sufficient. It's fast, easy to facilitate, and requires no specialist training. For significant incidents — LTIs, high-potential events, fatalities — you need a more structured approach like ICAM that explicitly surfaces organisational factors.

Consider Your Team's Experience

New investigation teams benefit from the Fishbone Diagram because the brainstorming structure ensures all cause categories are considered before analysis begins. Experienced teams who understand causal factor analysis will get more depth from ICAM's structured approach.

Know Your Regulatory Environment

Mining, oil & gas, and construction operations in Australia, Africa, and the Middle East increasingly require documented ICAM investigations for serious incidents. If your industry regulator expects ICAM output, use the ICAM template — don't try to reverse-engineer ICAM findings from a 5 Whys worksheet after the fact.

Don't Over-Engineer Minor Events

Running a full ICAM investigation on a minor first-aid incident wastes investigator time and trains people to resent the process. Reserve ICAM for incidents where organisational learning is genuinely valuable. For the majority of minor events, a well-executed 5 Whys with documented corrective actions is appropriate and effective.
