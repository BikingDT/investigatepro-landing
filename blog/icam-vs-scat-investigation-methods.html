ICAM vs SCAT: Which Incident Investigation Method Should You Use?

Both ICAM and SCAT are powerful incident investigation methodologies used by safety professionals worldwide. But they take fundamentally different approaches. Here's how to choose the right one for your organization.

What is ICAM?

ICAM (Incident Cause Analysis Method) is a systematic investigation methodology developed in Australia by the Minerals Council. It's designed to identify the organizational factors that contribute to incidents—not just the immediate causes.

ICAM is built on James Reason's "Swiss Cheese" model of accident causation. It recognizes that incidents rarely have a single cause. Instead, multiple failures must align—like holes in slices of Swiss cheese—for an incident to occur.

The ICAM Framework

ICAM uses a structured approach with five key elements:

  1. Absent or Failed Defences: The barriers that should have prevented the incident
  2. Individual/Team Actions: The actions (or inactions) that directly led to the incident
  3. Task/Environmental Conditions: The workplace conditions present at the time
  4. Organizational Factors: The systemic issues within the organization (using PEEPO categories)
  5. Risk Controls: Recommendations to prevent recurrence

💡 PEEPO Categories

ICAM uses PEEPO to categorize organizational factors: People, Environment, Equipment, Procedures, and Organization. This ensures investigators look beyond individual blame to systemic issues.

What is SCAT?

SCAT (Systematic Cause Analysis Technique) was developed by the International Loss Control Institute (ILCI) as part of the Bird-Germain loss causation model. It's one of the most widely used investigation methods globally.

SCAT uses a domino-effect model: incidents happen when a sequence of causes align, like falling dominoes. Remove one domino, and you break the chain.

The SCAT Framework

SCAT investigates incidents through four levels:

  1. Loss: The injury, damage, or business interruption
  2. Incident: The contact with energy or substance that caused the loss
  3. Immediate Causes: Substandard acts and conditions
  4. Basic Causes: Personal factors and job factors
  5. Lack of Control: Inadequate program, standards, or compliance

SCAT provides extensive checklists for each level, making it easier for investigators to identify contributing factors without missing anything.

Key Differences Between ICAM and SCAT

1. Philosophy

ICAM is explicitly "blame-free." It focuses on systemic failures, not individual fault. The methodology assumes that people generally try to do the right thing, and incidents occur due to organizational weaknesses.

SCAT identifies "substandard acts" as immediate causes, which can feel more blame-oriented. However, it does trace these back to system failures.

2. Structure

ICAM is more flexible. Investigators build a narrative and identify contributing factors organically, using the framework as a guide.

SCAT is more prescriptive. It provides detailed checklists with hundreds of potential causes. This is helpful for consistency but can feel mechanical.

3. Organizational Focus

ICAM puts heavy emphasis on organizational factors through PEEPO. It's designed to surface management system failures.

SCAT addresses organizational issues through "Lack of Control" but with less structured guidance.

When to Use Each Method

Choose ICAM When:

  • Investigating serious or complex incidents
  • Your organization wants to move away from blame culture
  • You need to identify systemic/management failures
  • Operating in high-risk industries (mining, construction, rail)
  • Regulatory requirements favor human factors analysis
  • You want investigations that drive organizational change

Choose SCAT When:

  • Investigating high volumes of incidents consistently
  • Your team has limited investigation experience
  • You need standardized, comparable data across sites
  • Quick turnaround is important
  • Your industry already uses SCAT widely (easier benchmarking)
  • You want checklist-driven thoroughness

Can You Combine ICAM and SCAT?

Absolutely. Many organizations use a tiered approach:

  • Minor incidents: Use SCAT checklists for quick, consistent analysis
  • Serious incidents: Use full ICAM methodology with PEEPO organizational analysis
  • High-potential incidents: Use ICAM regardless of actual severity

You can also use SCAT's detailed checklists as a prompt when conducting ICAM investigations. The two methods complement each other well.

Conclusion

Both ICAM and SCAT are effective incident investigation methodologies. The right choice depends on your organization's culture, industry, incident severity, and investigation resources.

Our recommendation: For organizations committed to learning from incidents and driving systemic improvement, ICAM's blame-free approach and organizational focus typically delivers better long-term results. SCAT's checklists can supplement ICAM investigations for thoroughness.
