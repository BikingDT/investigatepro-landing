Construction Site Incident Investigation Checklist (2026)

A field-ready checklist for Safety Managers and site HSE teams — covering every phase of a construction incident investigation from scene preservation to corrective action close-out.

Published May 5, 2026 • Updated May 5, 2026

Jump to Phase

Construction is consistently one of the world's most hazardous industries. Falls from height, caught-between incidents, crane and lifting operations, excavation collapses, and electrical contacts account for the majority of serious injuries and fatalities on construction sites globally.

When an incident happens on your site, the quality of your investigation determines whether the same event happens again. This checklist gives your team a structured path through every phase — from the moment something goes wrong to verified corrective action close-out.

Use it alongside a structured methodology like ICAM for serious incidents, or as a standalone guide for moderate-severity events.

Phase 1: Immediate Response (0–30 Minutes)

The actions taken in the first 30 minutes after an incident determine the quality of everything that follows. Get these right.

Phase 1 · Immediate

Immediate Response Checklist

Confirm the injured person(s) are receiving immediate medical careDon't delay treatment — medical response takes priority over all investigation activities
Call emergency services if required (ambulance, fire, rescue)
Establish the extent of the incident — are there other casualties or ongoing hazards?
Notify site manager / project manager immediately
Make the area safe — stop work in affected zone, control energy sources (lockout/tagout if applicable)
Notify the HSE/Safety Manager
Check regulatory notification requirements — serious injuries and fatalities have mandatory reporting timeframesTimeframes vary by jurisdiction: typically 1–24 hours for serious injuries, immediate for fatalities
Notify the principal contractor (if subcontractor incident)
Document the time of incident and time of notification

Phase 2: Scene Preservation (0–2 Hours)

Evidence degrades fast on construction sites — equipment gets moved, conditions change, and memory fades. Preserve the scene before anything is touched.

Phase 2 · Scene

Scene Preservation Checklist

Establish a physical exclusion zone around the incident areaNo entry without authorisation from the lead investigator
Post signage or barrier tape — make the exclusion zone visible to all site personnel
Photograph the scene from multiple angles before anything is movedWide shots first to capture context, then close-ups of specific evidence
Video the scene — a walkthrough recording captures spatial relationships that photos miss
Mark the positions of equipment, tools, and materials before any are moved
Identify and preserve physical evidence (tools, PPE, samples, broken components)
Check for CCTV coverage — request footage immediately before it's overwritten
Note weather conditions, lighting, temperature, time of day
Separate witnesses — prevent them from discussing the event before individual statements are taken
Record names and contact details of all witnesses
Collect relevant documents at the scene (permits, task cards, inspection records)

Phase 3: Investigation Team Assembly (2–4 Hours)

Phase 3 · Team

Team Assembly Checklist

Appoint a lead investigator — independent from the section where the incident occurred
Include a technical specialist relevant to the incident typeE.g. structural engineer for scaffold collapse, electrical engineer for electrocution, geotechnical for excavation failure
Include a worker representative — peer-level, builds trust with witnesses
Include an HSE representative to manage regulatory requirements
Confirm team members have no conflict of interest with the affected area or persons
Brief the team on the investigation scope, timeline, and expected outputs
Assign roles: lead investigator, evidence manager, witness interview coordinator, report writer
Confirm investigation methodology (ICAM recommended for serious incidents)

Phase 4: Evidence Collection (Days 1–3)

Construction incidents typically involve multiple evidence types. Capture all of them systematically.

Phase 4 · Evidence

Physical Evidence

Photograph all physical evidence with a scale reference (ruler, hard hat, etc.)
Measure and sketch the scene — distances, heights, positions
Collect failed components for engineering assessment (scaffolding clips, harness hardware, tool parts)
Preserve PPE worn at time of incident — harness, helmet, gloves
Document equipment condition: age, maintenance status, last inspection date
Check equipment data recorders or telematics if applicable (cranes, elevated work platforms)
Phase 4 · Evidence

Document Evidence

Collect the relevant SWMS (Safe Work Method Statement) or JSA for the task being performed
Obtain the work permit / permit to work if applicable
Collect pre-start inspection records for equipment involved
Obtain training and competency records for all involved workers
Review site induction records
Obtain scaffold inspection tags and records if scaffolding is involved
Review the toolbox talk record for the day of the incident
Obtain plant and equipment maintenance records
Review risk assessment for the task or area
Check CCTV and access control logs
Review recent incident, near miss, and hazard reports from the same area

Phase 5: Witness Interviews

Witness interviews are often the most critical and most poorly-executed part of construction investigations. Follow these principles.

Phase 5 · Interviews

Witness Interview Checklist

Interview witnesses individually — never in groups
Interview as soon as possible after the incident — memory degrades rapidly
Choose a private, neutral location away from the worksite
Explain the purpose: learning, not blame. Assure confidentiality where possible.
Start with open questions: "Tell me what happened from the beginning"
Let the witness give their full account before asking specific questions
Avoid leading questions — ask what happened, not "did you see him fall?"
Probe for: what was normal about the task, what was different that day
Ask about conditions: time pressure, fatigue, equipment issues, supervisor communication
Document responses accurately — record verbatim where possible
Ask the witness to review and sign their statement
Interview the supervisor(s) responsible for the area — separately from workers
Interview the injured person when medically cleared to do so

Phase 6: Root Cause Analysis

This is where most construction investigations fall short. Go beyond the immediate cause to organizational factors.

Phase 6 · Analysis

Root Cause Analysis Checklist

Build a timeline of events — what happened, in sequence, from normal work activity to the incident
Identify absent or failed defences: what controls should have prevented this but didn't?
Identify individual/team actions: what did people do or not do?
Identify task/environmental conditions: what made the task more difficult or more hazardous?
Identify organizational factors: what management decisions, system gaps, or resource issues created these conditions?This is the most important level — and the one most often skipped
Apply 5 Whys to each contributing factor — push past "human error" to systemic causes
Verify each causal factor with evidence — don't accept speculation as fact
Check: has this type of incident or near miss happened before on this site or within the company?
Consider whether this is a pSIF (potential serious injury or fatality) event requiring escalated analysis

Phase 7: Corrective Actions

Phase 7 · Actions

Corrective Action Checklist

For every causal factor, identify at least one corrective action that directly addresses it
Apply hierarchy of controls: prioritise elimination and engineering controls over administrative and PPE
Assign each action a specific owner (name, not just a role)
Set a realistic due date for each action
Define how completion will be verified — physical inspection, updated document, training record
Identify any immediate interim controls needed before permanent fixes are in place
Check if any actions have site-wide or company-wide implications — escalate if so
Enter all actions into your tracking system with owners and due dates

Phase 8: Report, Communication & Close-Out

Phase 8 · Close-Out

Reporting & Close-Out Checklist

Complete the formal investigation report — methodology, evidence, causal factors, corrective actions
Submit regulatory notification/report within required timeframe
Notify principal contractor and client as required by contract
Conduct a safety stand-down or toolbox talk to communicate learnings to the workforce
Share learnings with other sites or projects within the company
Update the SWMS or procedures affected by the investigation findings
Track corrective action completion — follow up with owners at due dates
Physically verify each action is implemented before marking complete
Conduct a follow-up review 30–60 days after close-out — has the risk actually been reduced?
Archive investigation records in a retrievable system — you'll need them for future trend analysis

Common Construction Incident Types — Special Considerations

Falls from Height

Falls are the leading cause of construction fatalities. When investigating, specifically examine: edge protection design and installation, harness inspection and anchor point adequacy, rescue plan (was there one?), and whether workers were working in proximity to unprotected edges as a routine practice or a one-off deviation.

Scaffold Failures

Check: scaffold design (was it engineered?), erector competency, inspection regime, modification history (was it modified after inspection sign-off?), and load management. Scaffold failures are almost always a system failure, not a random structural event.

Crane and Lifting Incidents

Examine: lift plan adequacy, exclusion zone management, load assessment, operator certification, signal communication, and ground conditions. The most common contributing factor is inadequate lift planning — particularly for complex or non-routine lifts.

Excavation Incidents

Investigate: soil classification, shoring/battering design, inspection regime, changing conditions (rain, vibration), and whether service locating was completed before excavation began.

Electrical Incidents

Focus on: isolation procedures, permit systems, cable detection, proximity to overhead lines, and whether electrical work was within the competency of the person performing it. Unauthorised electrical work is a recurring factor.
