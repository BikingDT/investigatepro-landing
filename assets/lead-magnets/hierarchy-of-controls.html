Hierarchy of
Controls

The Hierarchy of Controls ranks control measures by effectiveness. Always start at the top — higher controls don't rely on human behavior to work, making them more reliable at preventing incidents.
1

Elimination

Remove the hazard completely

Eliminate the task, remove the substance, redesign the process
2

Substitution

Replace with something less hazardous

Safer chemical, quieter equipment, automated process
3

Engineering Controls

Physically isolate people from the hazard

Guards, barriers, ventilation, interlocks, alarms
4

Administrative Controls

Change how people work

Procedures, training, signage, job rotation, supervision
5

PPE

Protect the individual worker

Safety glasses, gloves, hearing protection, harnesses
Most Effective
Least Effective

💡 Start at the Top

Always ask: "Can we eliminate or substitute first?" before defaulting to admin controls and PPE.

💡 Combine Controls

Multiple layers of control are often needed. Don't rely on a single measure.

💡 Human Behavior

Controls 4 & 5 require perfect human compliance every time. That's why they fail most often.

💡 Verify Effectiveness

After implementing controls, verify they actually work and are maintained over time.

❌ Common Corrective Action Mistakes

  • "Retrain all workers" (doesn't address root cause)
  • "Add more warning signs" (signs don't stop incidents)
  • "Remind people to be careful" (not actionable)
  • "Update the procedure" (if the old one wasn't followed, why will this one be?)
  • "Increase supervision" (vague and unsustainable)
  • "Discipline the worker" (doesn't fix the system)
