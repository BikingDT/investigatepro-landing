## Thread 2: PEEPO Framework Explained

**Tweet 1:**
The PEEPO framework is the simplest way to conduct thorough incident investigations.

Here's how it works (save this):

🧵

**Tweet 2:**
PEEPO stands for:

🔵 P - People
🔵 E - Environment
🔵 E - Equipment
🔵 P - Procedures
🔵 O - Organization

For every incident, you analyze contributing factors at each level.

**Tweet 3:**
P - PEOPLE

What human factors contributed?
• Fatigue, stress, distraction
• Training gaps
• Experience level
• Physical capabilities

But here's the key: these aren't ROOT causes. They're symptoms.

**Tweet 4:**
E - ENVIRONMENT

What physical conditions mattered?
• Lighting, noise, temperature
• Housekeeping
• Workspace layout
• Weather conditions

Ask: Did the environment set people up to succeed or fail?

**Tweet 5:**
E - EQUIPMENT

What tools and machinery were involved?
• Design flaws
• Maintenance state
• Guarding adequacy
• Ergonomic factors

Ask: Did the equipment work as it should? As it was designed?

**Tweet 6:**
P - PROCEDURES

What was the documented way to work?
• Did a procedure exist?
• Was it physically possible to follow?
• Was it known and understood?
• Did people follow it? Why or why not?

**Tweet 7:**
O - ORGANIZATION

This is where root causes live.

Every factor in P-E-E-P exists because of an organizational decision:
• Hiring decisions → People factors
• Facility decisions → Environment
• Procurement → Equipment
• Process design → Procedures

**Tweet 8:**
The power of PEEPO:

It ensures you consider every factor systematically.
It forces you past surface findings.
It traces everything back to organizational decisions.

That's where you can actually make change.

## Thread 4: Hierarchy of Controls

**Tweet 1:**
"Retrain all workers" is the laziest corrective action in safety.

Here's the Hierarchy of Controls and why it matters:

🧵

**Tweet 2:**
The Hierarchy of Controls ranks solutions by effectiveness:

🔴 Elimination (remove hazard)
🟠 Substitution (replace with safer)
🟡 Engineering (physical changes)
🟢 Administrative (procedures/training)
🔵 PPE (personal protection)

Top = most effective.

**Tweet 3:**
Here's the problem:

I reviewed 500 corrective actions from incident investigations.

85% were administrative or PPE.
10% were engineering.
5% were elimination or substitution.

We're defaulting to the WEAKEST controls.

**Tweet 4:**
Why do we default to weak controls?

• They're cheap
• They're fast
• They're easy to implement
• They look like action

But they depend entirely on human behavior. Every. Single. Time.

**Tweet 5:**
Here's the hierarchy in action:

Hazard: Workers getting hand injuries on machine

❌ PPE: "Require cut-resistant gloves"
❌ Admin: "Retrain on proper technique"
✅ Engineering: "Install guarding"
✅ Elimination: "Automate the task"

**Tweet 6:**
The rule:

For every corrective action, ask:
"Can we solve this at a higher tier?"

Can we eliminate? → No? Why not?
Can we substitute? → No? Why not?
Can we engineer? → No? Why not?

ONLY THEN consider admin/PPE.

**Tweet 7:**
Engineering costs more upfront.
Engineering costs less in incidents.

A $10K guard that prevents a $200K incident?
That's a 2,000% ROI.

"We can't afford it" usually means "we haven't calculated the alternative."

**Tweet 8:**
Challenge for your next investigation:

Push for at least ONE corrective action at Tier 1-3.

Not "more training."
Not "updated procedure."
Not "additional PPE."

Something that changes the SYSTEM.

## Thread 5: The Just Culture Myth

**Tweet 1:**
"We have a just culture."

No, you probably don't. Here's how to tell:

🧵

**Tweet 2:**
The Just Culture test:

When an incident happens, what's the FIRST question leadership asks?

A) "Who did this?"
B) "Is everyone okay?"
C) "What system failed?"

If it's A, you don't have a just culture. You have a blame culture with better PR.

**Tweet 3:**
Just Culture doesn't mean no accountability.

It means:
• Honest mistakes → Coaching
• At-risk behavior → System changes
• Reckless behavior → Discipline

The key: You have to actually distinguish between them.

**Tweet 4:**
Here's what kills Just Culture:

Outcome bias.

Same behavior, different outcomes:
• Worker takes shortcut, nothing happens → Ignored
• Worker takes shortcut, incident happens → Fired

The BEHAVIOR was the same. Only the luck was different.

**Tweet 5:**
Test your just culture:

Are near-misses treated the same as incidents?

If near-misses get "thanks for reporting" and incidents get "who's responsible" — you're punishing bad luck, not bad behavior.

**Tweet 6:**
The near-miss reporting rate tells the truth.

High reporting = People trust the system
Low reporting = People fear the consequences

If your near-miss numbers are low, people aren't reporting because they don't trust what happens next.

**Tweet 7:**
Building real Just Culture:

1. Leaders ask "what" before "who"
2. Same behavior = same response (regardless of outcome)
3. Near-misses celebrated, not ignored
4. Investigations find SYSTEMS to fix
5. Discipline is rare and clearly explained

**Tweet 8:**
The hardest part?

Leaders have to go first.

"What decision did WE make that contributed to this?"

Until leadership includes themselves in the investigation, it's not Just Culture. It's just words.

## Thread 6: Why Incidents Repeat

**Tweet 1:**
The same incident happened again.

Despite the investigation. Despite the corrective actions. Despite everything.

Here's why (and how to break the cycle):

🧵

**Tweet 2:**
Repeat incidents are a SYSTEM failure, not a people failure.

If the same incident recurs, your investigation didn't find the actual root cause.

Full stop.

**Tweet 3:**
The 5 reasons incidents repeat:

1. Stopped at human error
2. Weak corrective actions
3. Actions not completed
4. Wrong organizational factor identified
5. No cross-incident learning

Let's break each one down.

**Tweet 4:**
#1: Stopped at human error

"Worker didn't follow procedure" isn't a finding.
It's where you stopped investigating.

WHY didn't they follow it?
• Impossible to follow?
• Didn't know it existed?
• Time pressure made it impractical?

THAT'S your root cause.

**Tweet 5:**
#2: Weak corrective actions

"Retrain workers" fails because:
• The system that caused the error still exists
• Training addresses knowledge, not ability
• Muscle memory beats classroom memory

If you can retrain your way out of it, it wasn't a system problem.

**Tweet 6:**
#3: Actions not completed

The investigation is useless if corrective actions don't happen.

I've seen:
• 40% of actions "pending" after 90 days
• 25% quietly closed with "no longer relevant"
• 15% never assigned to anyone

Track. Escalate. Verify.

**Tweet 7:**
#4: Wrong organizational factor

You found AN organizational factor.
But was it THE one that mattered?

Pattern to watch: You keep fixing things, incidents keep happening.

You're treating symptoms, not causes.

**Tweet 8:**
#5: No cross-incident learning

Each investigation exists in isolation.
No one checks if similar incidents happened before.
No one connects the dots across sites/shifts/time.

Organizational memory doesn't exist.

**Tweet 9:**
Breaking the cycle:

✅ Ask "why" 5 times (minimum)
✅ Push for engineering controls
✅ Track every action to completion
✅ Link new incidents to past ones
✅ Review patterns monthly

The incidents you prevent are the ones you never see.

## Thread 9: The Investigation Timeline

**Tweet 1:**
Your investigation shouldn't take 3 weeks.

Here's what a systematic timeline looks like:

🧵

**Tweet 2:**
The typical timeline (broken):

Day 1-3: Figure out what happened
Day 4-7: Wait for people to "find time"
Day 8-14: Analysis (with interruptions)
Day 15-21: Write the report
Day 22-30: Reviews and approvals
Day 31+: Maybe assign actions

Total: 4-6 weeks. If you're lucky.

**Tweet 3:**
The systematic timeline:

Hour 0-4: Immediate data collection
Hour 4-24: PEEPO analysis
Hour 24-36: Root cause identification
Hour 36-48: Report + actions assigned

Same quality. Different methodology.

**Tweet 4:**
Hour 0-4: Data collection

• Secure the scene
• Photos/video of everything
• Initial witness statements
• Timeline of events
• Evidence preservation

Don't analyze yet. Just collect.

**Tweet 5:**
Hour 4-24: PEEPO analysis

• Review all data
• Identify factors at each PEEPO level
• Interview witnesses with targeted questions
• Map contributing factors
• Start organizational factor tracing

**Tweet 6:**
Hour 24-36: Root cause identification

• Trace each factor to organizational decisions
• Apply 5-Whys to key findings
• Identify absent/failed defenses
• Prioritize root causes by significance

**Tweet 7:**
Hour 36-48: Close out

• Draft report with findings
• Assign SMART corrective actions
• Route for approval
• Communicate learnings
• Schedule follow-up verification

**Tweet 8:**
"But we can't move that fast!"

Yes, you can. With:
• Standardized data collection
• Guided analysis (no blank page)
• Built-in rigor (can't skip steps)
• Immediate action assignment

Speed and rigor aren't trade-offs.

**Tweet 9:**
The 48-hour investigation isn't a dream.

It's what systematic methodology looks like.

Your investigation quality determines your prevention quality.

Both should be fast AND thorough.
