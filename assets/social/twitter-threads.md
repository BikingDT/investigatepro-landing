## Thread 1: Why Incident Investigations Fail

**Hook Tweet:**
80% of incident investigations fail to prevent recurrence.

Here's why — and how to fix it:

🧵👇

---

**Tweet 2:**
The #1 problem: investigations are treated as COMPLIANCE, not LEARNING.

When the goal is to complete the form and close the case, you get shallow analysis.

When the goal is to genuinely prevent recurrence, you get real results.

---

**Tweet 3:**
Symptom of compliance mindset:

• Root cause: "Worker failed to follow procedure"
• Corrective action: "Retrain all workers"

Sound familiar?

Neither of these will prevent the next incident.

---

**Tweet 4:**
The fix: always ask "why was this possible?"

Worker didn't follow procedure → WHY?
• Was it practical?
• Was there time?
• Was there pressure?
• Were they trained?

Human error is a SYMPTOM, not a cause.

---

**Tweet 5:**
Another killer: weak corrective actions.

❌ "Add warning signs"
❌ "Remind people to be careful"
❌ "Update the procedure"

These rely on perfect human behavior. Forever.

That's not how humans work.

---

**Tweet 6:**
Use the Hierarchy of Controls instead:

1. Elimination (remove hazard)
2. Substitution (replace with safer)
3. Engineering (physical barriers)
4. Administrative (procedures, training)
5. PPE (personal protection)

Start at the top. Don't default to #4 and #5.

---

**Tweet 7:**
The biggest miss: not reaching ORGANIZATIONAL factors.

Equipment failed → who decided maintenance schedules?
Procedure was impractical → who wrote it? When?
Worker was rushed → who set the targets?

That's where root causes live.

---

**Tweet 8:**
Final mistake: investigating in isolation.

Each incident goes in its own file. Case closed.

But incidents aren't isolated. They're symptoms of systemic weaknesses.

Look for PATTERNS across multiple investigations.

---

**Tweet 9:**
TL;DR:

✅ Investigate to LEARN, not just comply
✅ Human error isn't a root cause
✅ Use hierarchy of controls
✅ Always reach organizational factors
✅ Look for patterns

Fix the system, not just the symptoms.

## Thread 2: The PEEPO Framework in 60 Seconds

**Hook Tweet:**
Most investigators focus on WHAT went wrong.

The PEEPO framework asks: What ALLOWED it to go wrong?

Here's the complete breakdown:

🧵👇

---

**Tweet 2:**
P — PEOPLE

Human factors that contributed:
• Fatigue / physical state
• Stress / mental state
• Knowledge / competence
• Communication

But remember: human error explains HOW, not WHY.

Always dig deeper.

---

**Tweet 3:**
E — ENVIRONMENT

Physical conditions that set the stage:
• Weather / lighting / noise
• Housekeeping / layout
• Time of day / shift
• Remote location

Even skilled workers fail in hostile environments.

---

**Tweet 4:**
E — EQUIPMENT

Tools and machinery factors:
• Design suitability
• Maintenance condition
• Guards / interlocks
• PPE availability

Equipment doesn't maintain itself. Who decided the maintenance schedule?

---

**Tweet 5:**
P — PROCEDURES

The documented way vs reality:
• Does it exist?
• Is it practical?
• Is it followed?

Key insight: if workers routinely deviate from procedures, the PROCEDURE is the problem.

---

**Tweet 6:**
O — ORGANIZATION

This is where ROOT CAUSES live:
• Management decisions
• Resource allocation
• Culture / priorities
• Competing pressures

Every P-E-E-P factor traces back here.

Don't stop until you find it.

---

**Tweet 7:**
The power move:

For every factor you find, ask:

"What ORGANIZATIONAL decision created this condition?"

That question transforms investigations from blame-finding to system-fixing.

## Posting Tips for Twitter/X

1. **Thread timing:** Post hook tweet, then rapid-fire the rest (1 per minute)
2. **Self-reply:** Always reply to yourself, don't create new tweets
3. **Engagement:** First hour matters most — engage with every reply
4. **Best times:** Weekday mornings (7-9am) in your target timezone
5. **Repurpose:** Turn threads into LinkedIn posts, newsletter content
6. **Images:** Add visuals to the hook tweet for higher engagement
