## Post 1: The Blame Game (Contrarian)

The blame game doesn't prevent incidents.

I've seen it hundreds of times:

Incident happens → Investigation → "Human error" → Retraining → Same incident happens again.

The cycle continues because we're answering the wrong question.

"Who made the mistake?" leads to blame.
"What allowed the mistake to happen?" leads to prevention.

Every human error traces back to organizational factors:
- Procedures that don't match reality
- Time pressure that makes shortcuts rational
- Equipment that sets people up to fail
- Training that doesn't address actual work conditions

The person who made the "error" is usually trying to get the job done despite the system — not because they're careless.

When you shift from WHO to WHY, two things happen:

1. People actually report incidents (no fear of punishment)
2. You fix things that prevent the NEXT 100 incidents

Stop investigating to find blame.
Start investigating to find systems to improve.

The incidents you prevent are the ones you never have to investigate.

## Post 2: What I Learned (Authority)

What I learned from reviewing 1,000+ incident investigations:

90% stop at the surface.

Here's what I mean:

The investigation finds "contributing factors" — things like:
- Worker didn't follow procedure
- Supervisor wasn't present
- Communication breakdown

All true. All useless for prevention.

Because they don't answer: WHY did this happen?

The 10% that actually prevent incidents dig deeper:

- WHY didn't the worker follow procedure? (It was physically impossible with available tools)
- WHY wasn't the supervisor present? (Managing 3x the recommended span of control)
- WHY did communication break down? (No standard handoff process exists)

Every surface finding has an organizational root cause:
- Procurement decisions
- Staffing decisions
- Process design decisions
- Training investment decisions

Leaders don't make "errors." They make DECISIONS.

Those decisions create conditions where incidents become likely — or unlikely.

The best investigators I've worked with have one thing in common:

They refuse to stop at human error.

## Post 4: Why RCA Fails (Educational)

Why root cause analysis fails (and how to fix it):

Root cause analysis has a branding problem.

People think it means: Find THE root cause.

But incidents don't have A root cause. They have MULTIPLE contributing factors across MULTIPLE system levels.

Here's how ICAM (Incident Cause Analysis Method) frames it:

**Level 1: Individual/Team Actions**
What did people do (or not do)?
→ But these aren't root causes. They're symptoms.

**Level 2: Task/Environmental Conditions**
What conditions influenced those actions?
→ Getting warmer. Still not root causes.

**Level 3: Organizational Factors**
What decisions, systems, or culture created those conditions?
→ THIS is where root causes live.

The fix:

For every surface finding, ask "Why did the system allow this?"

Keep asking until you reach a decision someone made (or didn't make).

That decision is your root cause.
That decision is something you can actually change.

"Worker rushed" → Why? → Time pressure → Why? → Understaffed → Why? → Budget decision

Now you have something actionable.

The worker wasn't the problem. The staffing decision was.

## Post 5: ICAM in 60 Seconds (Quick Value)

ICAM methodology explained in 60 seconds:

**What:** Incident Cause Analysis Method — a systematic approach to incident investigation

**Why:** Because "human error" isn't a root cause, it's where lazy investigations stop

**The Framework:**

1️⃣ **Gather Data**
Timeline, evidence, interviews. Facts only. No assumptions.

2️⃣ **PEEPO Analysis**
People, Environment, Equipment, Procedures, Organization
What factors contributed at each level?

3️⃣ **Absent/Failed Defenses**
What controls should have prevented this? Why didn't they work?

4️⃣ **Organizational Factors**
Trace every finding back to organizational decisions

5️⃣ **SMART Actions**
Specific, Measurable, Assignable, Realistic, Time-bound corrective actions

**The Key Insight:**

Every People, Environment, Equipment, and Procedure factor exists because of an ORGANIZATIONAL decision.

Fix the system, not the symptom.

**Save this post.** Use it on your next investigation.

## Post 6: 5 Signs (Diagnostic)

5 signs your investigation process is broken:

1️⃣ **"Human error" appears in >50% of findings**
That's not a finding. That's where you stopped digging.

2️⃣ **Corrective actions are mostly "retrain workers"**
Training doesn't fix broken systems. Hierarchy of Controls isn't optional.

3️⃣ **You see the same incident types year after year**
Recurring incidents = recurring system failures you haven't addressed.

4️⃣ **Investigations take 3+ weeks to close**
Complexity is an excuse. Good methodology simplifies, not complicates.

5️⃣ **Leadership asks "who's responsible?" first**
That question shapes every answer that follows. And not in a good way.

If you checked 3+ boxes, you don't have an investigation problem.

You have a METHODOLOGY problem.

The good news: methodology is fixable.

It's not about working harder. It's about working systematically.

ICAM exists. The tools exist. The knowledge exists.

The only question is whether you're ready to adopt them.

## Post 7: Incident to Insight (Outcome Focused)

From incident to insight in 48 hours.

That's not a dream. That's what systematic investigation looks like.

The typical timeline:

Day 1-3: Figure out what happened (gather data)
Day 4-7: Wait for people to "find time" to investigate
Day 8-14: Analysis (with interruptions)
Day 15-21: Write the report
Day 22-30: Reviews and approvals
Day 31+: Maybe assign corrective actions

Total: 4-6 weeks. If you're lucky.

The systematic timeline:

Hour 0-4: Immediate data collection (photos, statements, evidence)
Hour 4-24: PEEPO analysis (with guided prompts)
Hour 24-36: Root cause identification (tracing to organizational factors)
Hour 36-48: Report + corrective actions assigned

Same quality. Different methodology.

The secret isn't working faster. It's:
- Standardized data collection (nothing missed)
- Guided analysis (no blank page syndrome)
- Built-in rigor (can't skip steps)
- Immediate action assignment (no report-then-wait)

Your investigation quality determines your prevention quality.

Speed and rigor aren't trade-offs. With the right system, you get both.

## Post 8: Organizational Factors (Provocative)

The organizational factors no one wants to discuss:

Every incident investigation should end with an uncomfortable question:

"What decision did WE make that allowed this?"

Not the worker.
Not the supervisor.
WE. Leadership.

The factors everyone avoids:

📌 **Budgeting decisions**
"We can't afford proper tooling" → Workers adapt with what they have

📌 **Staffing decisions**
"We'll run lean this quarter" → Corners get cut

📌 **Scheduling decisions**
"We need this done by Friday" → Safety takes a back seat

📌 **Procurement decisions**
"Buy the cheaper option" → Equipment creates hazards

📌 **Cultural decisions**
"Don't bring me problems" → Issues go unreported

Investigations that stop at "worker error" are investigations that protect leadership from accountability.

That's not safety management. That's reputation management.

Real safety leadership means asking: "What did I do — or not do — that contributed to this?"

It's uncomfortable.
It's necessary.
It's how you actually prevent incidents.

## Post 9: Corrective Actions That Work (Practical)

Corrective actions that actually work:

The Hierarchy of Controls isn't a suggestion. It's a blueprint.

Tier 1 (Most Effective):
🔴 **Elimination** — Remove the hazard entirely
🟠 **Substitution** — Replace with something less hazardous

Tier 2 (Engineering):
🟡 **Engineering Controls** — Physically change the workspace

Tier 3 (Administrative):
🟢 **Administrative Controls** — Change how work is done
🔵 **PPE** — Last resort personal protection

The problem:

90% of corrective actions I see are Tier 3.

"Retrain workers."
"Update the procedure."
"Add a checklist."
"Wear additional PPE."

These are the WEAKEST controls. They depend entirely on human behavior.

The fix:

For every corrective action, ask: "Can we solve this at a higher tier?"

Can we eliminate the hazard? → No? Why not?
Can we substitute? → No? Why not?
Can we engineer it out? → No? Why not?

Only THEN consider administrative controls.

Engineering controls cost more upfront. They cost less in incidents.

Stop defaulting to "training" as a fix. Start defaulting to "How do we design this hazard out of existence?"

## Post 10: AI in Safety (Timely/Trendy)

AI in safety: help or hype?

I've been in safety for 15+ years. I've seen trends come and go.

AI is different. But not for the reasons you might think.

What AI WON'T do:
❌ Replace safety professionals
❌ Investigate incidents autonomously
❌ Make judgment calls about risk
❌ Understand your organizational culture

What AI CAN do:
✅ Guide investigators through methodology (step by step)
✅ Suggest questions they might not think to ask
✅ Flag patterns across hundreds of investigations
✅ Catch weak corrective actions before they're approved
✅ Make ICAM accessible to non-experts

The real opportunity:

Most organizations don't have enough ICAM-trained investigators.

AI doesn't replace expertise. It DISTRIBUTES it.

A supervisor with AI guidance can conduct a better investigation than an untrained investigator working alone.

That's not replacing humans. That's augmenting them.

The companies that will win at safety aren't the ones ignoring AI.

They're the ones asking: "How do we use AI to multiply our team's capability?"

The future of safety isn't AI vs. humans.

It's AI + humans vs. the same old incidents.
