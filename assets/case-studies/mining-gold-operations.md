# Case Study: Gold Mining Operations

## How a Major Gold Mining Company Cut Investigation Time by 60% and Reduced Repeat Incidents by 40%

**Industry:** Mining (Gold)  
**Company Size:** 8,500 employees across 12 sites  
**Region:** Africa & Middle East  

---

## The Challenge

A multinational gold mining company was struggling with their incident investigation process despite a strong commitment to safety.

### The Problems:

**Inconsistent Investigation Quality**
- Different sites used different methodologies
- Some investigators stopped at "human error"
- Root causes varied wildly in depth and quality

**Slow Turnaround**
- Average investigation took 18 days to close
- Critical learnings delayed by weeks
- Similar incidents occurred at other sites before investigations completed

**Weak Corrective Actions**
- 70% of actions were "retrain workers" or "update procedure"
- Corrective actions weren't tracked to completion
- Leadership lacked confidence in the data

**Recurring Incidents**
- Equipment-related hand injuries: 12 incidents in 18 months
- Vehicle incidents: 8 similar events across 4 sites
- Same root causes appearing site after site

> "We were investigating to check a box, not to learn. Every site did it differently, and we had no way to see patterns across the organization."
> 
> — **Regional Safety Director**

---

## The Solution

The company implemented InvestigatePro across all 12 sites, standardizing on the ICAM methodology with AI-guided investigation support.

### Implementation Approach:

**Phase 1: Pilot (2 sites, 6 weeks)**
- Selected one high-incident site and one high-performer
- Trained 12 investigators on ICAM + InvestigatePro
- Ran parallel investigations to validate approach

**Phase 2: Regional Rollout (8 weeks)**
- Deployed to remaining 10 sites
- Train-the-trainer model for scalability
- Integrated with existing safety management system

**Phase 3: Optimization (Ongoing)**
- Refined investigation templates for mining-specific scenarios
- Built equipment and location libraries
- Established cross-site learning reviews

### Key Features Utilized:

✅ **PEEPO Analysis Guidance** — AI prompts ensured every factor was considered  
✅ **Organizational Factor Tracking** — Forced investigations beyond surface findings  
✅ **Corrective Action Hierarchy** — Pushed teams toward engineering controls  
✅ **Cross-Site Pattern Recognition** — Identified recurring issues across operations  
✅ **Real-Time Dashboards** — Leadership visibility without chasing reports

## Key Learnings

### What Made It Work:

1. **Leadership Commitment**
   - Executive sponsor championed the change
   - Investigation quality became a KPI
   - Monthly cross-site learning reviews established

2. **Methodology Before Technology**
   - Teams understood WHY before HOW
   - ICAM training preceded software rollout
   - Focus on investigation quality, not just speed

3. **AI as Augmentation**
   - Investigators remained in control
   - AI prompted questions, didn't answer them
   - Experienced investigators found new insights

4. **Cross-Site Learning**
   - Pattern recognition revealed systemic issues
   - Best practices shared automatically
   - Reduced "not invented here" resistance

### What They'd Do Differently:

> "We should have started with our most skeptical site. Once they converted, everyone else followed. Instead, we started with our best site and had to overcome 'we're already good' pushback elsewhere."
>
> — **VP of Health & Safety**

## The Organizational Factors They Found

The most significant finding wasn't any single incident — it was the patterns across investigations:

**Procurement Decisions**
- Tool specifications didn't match actual work conditions
- "Lowest cost" bidding created equipment hazards
- 4 different equipment-related incidents traced to same vendor

**Supervision Ratios**
- Night shift had 3x the incident rate
- Root cause: Supervisor span of control (1:45 vs recommended 1:15)
- Resolved through staffing adjustment, not "more supervision training"

**Procedure Design**
- Critical procedures written by engineers who'd never done the task
- Workers had developed workarounds that were actually safer
- Procedures updated based on actual work practices
