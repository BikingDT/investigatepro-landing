# Case Study: Automotive Parts Manufacturing

## How a Tier-1 Automotive Supplier Achieved 50% Reduction in Repeat Incidents and Won "Supplier Safety Excellence" Award

**Industry:** Manufacturing (Automotive Parts)  
**Company Size:** 1,800 employees across 3 plants  
**Region:** Midwest United States (MI, OH, IN)  

---

## The Challenge

A Tier-1 automotive parts supplier was struggling to meet the increasingly stringent safety requirements of major OEM customers while managing a legacy paper-based investigation system.

### The Problems:

**Customer Pressure**
- Major OEM required quarterly safety metrics reporting
- "Supplier Safety Scorecard" scores declining
- Risk of losing $40M annual contract

**Paper-Based Limitations**
- Filing cabinets full of investigation forms
- No way to search historical incidents
- Each plant operated independently

**Repeat Incidents**
- Same machine-related injuries year after year
- "Retrain the operator" was default solution
- No organizational memory across shifts

**Leadership Disconnect**
- Plant managers never saw investigation details
- Corporate got summary numbers, not insights
- No data to drive investment decisions

> "We had 10 years of investigations in boxes. When a new incident happened, nobody checked if we'd seen it before. We were learning the same lessons over and over."
> 
> — **EHS Manager, Plant 2**

---

## The Solution

The company selected InvestigatePro to digitize their investigation process and create a unified learning system across all three manufacturing plants.

### Implementation Approach:

**Phase 1: Digital Migration (6 weeks)**
- Scanned and tagged 200+ historical investigations
- Built machine/equipment library specific to their operations
- Created custom investigation templates for manufacturing hazards

**Phase 2: Process Redesign (4 weeks)**
- Trained all supervisors on ICAM methodology
- Established shift-based investigation responsibilities
- Created escalation workflow to plant and corporate levels

**Phase 3: Culture Change (Ongoing)**
- Launched "Investigation Quality" recognition program
- Monthly cross-plant learning reviews
- Connected investigation data to capital investment decisions

### Key Features Utilized:

✅ **Historical Tagging** — Linked new incidents to similar past events  
✅ **Machine/Equipment Library** — Tracked incident patterns by specific assets  
✅ **Shift Handoff Integration** — Investigations didn't die between shifts  
✅ **Hierarchy of Controls Scoring** — Measured corrective action quality  
✅ **Customer Reporting** — Generated OEM-required documentation automatically

## Key Learnings

### What Made It Work:

1. **Historical Context**
   - Digitizing past investigations created instant value
   - New incidents automatically linked to similar events
   - "Organizational memory" finally existed

2. **Shift-Based Ownership**
   - Each shift owned their incidents
   - Handoff protocols ensured continuity
   - Competition between shifts drove quality

3. **Data-Driven Investment**
   - Investigation data identified high-risk equipment
   - Capital budget prioritized based on incident patterns
   - ROI became measurable for safety investments

4. **Customer Alignment**
   - OEM reporting automated (huge time savings)
   - Shared investigation methodology with customer auditors
   - Safety became competitive advantage, not cost center

### What They'd Do Differently:

> "We waited too long to involve the union. Once they saw we weren't using investigations for discipline — just improvement — they became our biggest advocates. The near-miss reporting explosion came from hourly workers feeling safe to report."
>
> — **Plant Manager, Plant 1**

## The Organizational Factors They Found

Cross-plant analysis revealed systemic issues that individual investigations had missed:

**Maintenance Scheduling**
- 35% of incidents occurred within 48 hours of PM cycles
- Lockout/tagout procedures didn't account for partial maintenance
- Revised PM protocols to include enhanced verification steps

**Training Gap**
- New employees had 4x incident rate in first 90 days
- Training focused on production, not hazard recognition
- Implemented "Safety First 90" program with mentorship

**Equipment Design**
- Three specific press models involved in 70% of hand injuries
- Guards had been removed for "productivity" years ago
- Engineering controls reinstalled with performance data proving no output loss

**Incentive Misalignment**
- Production bonuses inadvertently discouraged incident reporting
- Supervisors feared safety metrics would hurt their teams
- Restructured incentives to reward reporting, not just outcomes
