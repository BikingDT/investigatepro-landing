## Email 2: Day 2
**Subject:** The #1 investigation mistake (and how to avoid it)

---

Hi {{first_name}},

Quick question: Have you ever written "worker failed to follow procedure" as a root cause?

If so, you're not alone. It's the most common investigation finding.

It's also the reason incidents keep recurring.

**Here's the problem:** Human error explains HOW something happened. It doesn't explain WHY it was possible.

When you identify a human error, the investigation is just beginning. Ask:

- Was the procedure practical to follow?
- Was there time pressure?
- Were they properly trained?
- Was deviation normalized?
- What organizational decision created these conditions?

The real root causes are almost always upstream — in the systems, resources, and culture that shape the work environment.

**In your toolkit:** Pages 14-15 walk you through organizational factors. Don't skip them.

Tomorrow I'll share the hierarchy of controls — the framework for corrective actions that actually work.

## Email 3: Day 4
**Subject:** Why "retrain all workers" never works

---

Hi {{first_name}},

Let's talk corrective actions.

After analyzing thousands of investigations, I can predict the most common corrective actions:

❌ "Retrain all workers"  
❌ "Update the procedure"  
❌ "Add warning signs"  
❌ "Remind people to be more careful"  

These all have something in common: they rely on perfect human behavior, forever.

That's not how humans work.

**The Hierarchy of Controls** ranks measures by effectiveness:

1. **Elimination** — Remove the hazard (most effective)
2. **Substitution** — Replace with less hazardous
3. **Engineering** — Physical barriers, guards, interlocks
4. **Administrative** — Procedures, training, signage
5. **PPE** — Personal protective equipment (least effective)

Most corrective actions cluster at levels 4 and 5. That's why incidents repeat.

**Challenge:** For your next investigation, ask "Can we eliminate or engineer this?" before defaulting to retraining.

**In your toolkit:** The Corrective Action Planner (pages 19-20) includes a hierarchy of controls quality check.

## Email 4: Day 7
**Subject:** Are you investigating in isolation?

---

Hi {{first_name}},

Here's a question most safety teams can't answer:

"How many incidents in the last 12 months involved shift handover communication issues?"

Or contractor management. Or time pressure. Or inadequate risk assessment.

If you can't answer immediately, you're investigating in isolation.

**The pattern problem:** Organizations investigate each incident as if it's unique. Case closed. Filed away.

But incidents aren't isolated events. They're symptoms of systemic weaknesses that show up again and again in different forms.

When you can't see patterns, you can't fix systems. You just keep playing whack-a-mole.

**The fix:**
- Tag contributing factors consistently
- Review trends quarterly
- When the same organizational factor appears in multiple incidents, address it at the systemic level

This is where investigation software helps. Manual systems make pattern detection nearly impossible.
