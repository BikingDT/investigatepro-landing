# LinkedIn Talking Head Video Scripts

**Format:** Direct-to-camera, founder/expert talking
**Duration:** 60-90 seconds each
**Purpose:** LinkedIn organic, thought leadership, personal brand

---

## Video 1: "Human Error Is Not a Root Cause"

**Duration:** 60 seconds

---

**[Looking at camera, conversational tone]**

"Human error is not a root cause.

I know that sounds controversial. But hear me out.

When your investigation concludes with 'human error,' you haven't found the root cause. You've found where you STOPPED looking.

The real question isn't 'who made a mistake?' It's 'what ALLOWED the mistake to happen?'

Was the procedure impossible to follow? Was there time pressure from production? Was the equipment poorly designed?

Every human error traces back to an organizational decision — about budgets, staffing, procedures, equipment.

THAT'S where root causes live. THAT'S what you can actually change.

Next time your investigation lands on 'human error,' don't stop there. Ask why. Then ask why again. Keep going until you reach a decision someone made.

That's how you prevent the NEXT incident. Not by blaming the last person who touched it."

**[End with slight nod, confident expression]**

## Video 2: "Why Incidents Keep Recurring"

**Duration:** 75 seconds

---

**[Direct to camera]**

"Let me tell you why the same incidents keep happening at your organization.

It's not because your people don't learn. It's not because they don't care.

It's because your investigations aren't finding the real root causes.

Here's what I see over and over:

Incident happens. Investigation finds 'human error.' Corrective action: 'retrain workers.' Six months later? Same incident.

The cycle repeats because retraining doesn't fix broken systems.

If your procedure is impossible to follow — training won't help.
If your equipment creates hazards — training won't help.
If production pressure forces shortcuts — training won't help.

The only way to break the cycle is to trace every finding back to organizational factors. The decisions about budgets, staffing, procedures, and equipment that created the conditions for the incident.

Fix THOSE, and you stop the cycle.

Keep blaming workers, and you'll be investigating the same incident next year."

**[Pause, then slight smile]**

"Your choice."

## Video 3: "The $200,000 Question"

**Duration:** 60 seconds

---

**[Holding up hand, casual but direct]**

"Quick question.

How much does a recordable incident cost your organization?

If you said forty thousand dollars, you're not wrong. That's the direct cost.

But indirect costs? Four to ten times higher.

Investigation time. Production disruption. Equipment damage. Hiring. Training. Legal. Insurance.

A single recordable can cost two hundred thousand dollars or more.

Now here's the real question:

What percentage of your incidents are REPEAT incidents? Same root cause, different day?

Industry average is thirty to forty percent.

That means a third of your incident costs are PREVENTABLE. If your investigations actually found — and fixed — the real root causes.

For most organizations, that's hundreds of thousands of dollars. Every year.

The cost of better investigations? A few thousand.

The math isn't close."

**[Slight pause]**

"Are your investigations worth the investment?"

## Video 4: "Retrain Workers Is Not a Fix"

**Duration:** 75 seconds

---

**[Shaking head slightly, then looking at camera]**

"'Retrain all workers.'

If that's your go-to corrective action, we need to talk.

Training is the WEAKEST control in the Hierarchy of Controls. It depends on human behavior, every single time.

And here's the thing — the worker you're retraining probably knew the right way. They didn't follow the procedure because:

The procedure was impossible to follow.
Or time pressure made shortcuts rational.
Or the equipment made the safe way harder than the unsafe way.

Training doesn't fix any of those problems.

You know what does?

Engineering controls. Physical changes that make the wrong way impossible.

Elimination. Removing the hazard entirely.

Substitution. Replacing dangerous processes with safer ones.

These work even when people make mistakes. Because people WILL make mistakes.

Next time you're about to write 'retrain workers' as a corrective action, stop.

Ask yourself: 'What engineering control would make this mistake impossible?'

If you can't eliminate the hazard, you haven't finished investigating."

## Video 5: "The Question That Changes Investigations"

**Duration:** 60 seconds

---

**[Leaning in slightly, like sharing a secret]**

"I'm going to give you one question that will transform your incident investigations.

Ready?

'What decision did WE make that contributed to this?'

Not the worker. Not the supervisor. WE. Leadership.

This is uncomfortable. I know.

But every incident traces back to organizational decisions:

Budgeting decisions that limited resources.
Staffing decisions that created pressure.
Procurement decisions that bought inadequate equipment.
Cultural decisions that discouraged reporting.

When you start asking what LEADERSHIP contributed, you stop blaming workers and start fixing systems.

That's the shift from investigation for ACCOUNTABILITY to investigation for LEARNING.

One finds someone to punish. The other prevents the next hundred incidents.

Which one do you want?"

**[Hold gaze for a beat]**

## Production Tips

**Setup:**
- Good natural lighting (face a window)
- Clean, professional background (office, bookshelf)
- Eye level camera (stack books under laptop if needed)
- Look at camera lens, not screen

**Delivery:**
- Conversational, not scripted-sounding
- Okay to pause, gesture, think
- Energy without being salesy
- Authenticity > perfection

**Technical:**
- Shoot vertical (9:16) for LinkedIn native
- Also shoot horizontal for repurposing
- Use phone on tripod or webcam
- Lapel mic or close to camera mic

**Posting:**
- Upload native (not YouTube links)
- Add captions (80% watch without sound)
- First line of caption = hook
- Include relevant hashtags
